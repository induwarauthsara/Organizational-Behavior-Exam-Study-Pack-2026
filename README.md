<summary><strong>1. Personality &amp; Big Five</strong></summary>

### The Big Five Personality Traits (OCEAN)

| Trait | Description | High Score | Low Score | IT Workplace Example |
|-------|-------------|-----------|-----------|----------------------|
| **Openness** | Curiosity, creativity, willingness to try new things | Innovative, creative, adaptable | Conventional, prefers routine | A developer who eagerly adopts new frameworks and proposes novel architecture solutions |
| **Conscientiousness** | Organized, dependable, self-disciplined | Reliable, goal-oriented, thorough | Disorganized, careless, impulsive | A project manager who meticulously tracks deadlines and documents every sprint task |
| **Extraversion** | Sociable, assertive, talkative | Outgoing, energetic, seeks stimulation | Introverted, reserved, independent | A team lead who energizes daily stand-ups and actively networks at tech conferences |
| **Agreeableness** | Cooperative, trusting, empathetic | Helpful, compassionate, conflict-averse | Competitive, suspicious, blunt | A QA engineer who collaborates warmly with developers rather than pointing blame |
| **Neuroticism** | Emotional instability, anxiety, moodiness | Anxious, moody, easily stressed | Calm, resilient, emotionally stable | A support engineer who remains composed during production outages vs. one who panics |

### MBTI vs. Big Five Comparison

| Dimension | MBTI | Big Five |
|-----------|------|----------|
| **Basis** | Jungian theory; dichotomous types | Factor analysis; continuous spectra |
| **Output** | 16 personality types (e.g., INTJ, ENFP) | Five independent trait scores |
| **Reliability** | Low test-retest reliability (~50% retype) | High test-retest reliability |
| **Scientific support** | Limited | Extensive empirical support |
| **Use in OB** | Team-building, self-awareness exercises | Predicting job performance, leadership |
| **Example** | An INTJ may prefer working independently | High Conscientiousness predicts high job performance |

### Designing a Personality Assessment Tool for an IT Company

1. **Define the purpose** — e.g., hiring, team composition, leadership development
2. **Select validated instruments** — use Big Five inventories (NEO-PI-R, BFI) rather than unvalidated tools
3. **Administer reliably** — standardized conditions, online platform with proctoring for hiring
4. **Interpret contextually** — combine with technical tests; no single trait disqualifies a candidate
5. **Ensure ethical compliance** — obtain consent, maintain confidentiality, avoid discrimination
6. **Provide feedback** — share results with candidates/employees for development purposes

<summary><strong>2. Perception &amp; Attribution</strong></summary>

### Six Common Perceptual Errors

| Error | Definition | IT Workplace Example |
|-------|-----------|----------------------|
| **Stereotyping** | Judging based on group membership | "Developers are bad communicators" — ignoring individual differences |
| **Halo Effect** | One positive trait colours all judgements | A brilliant coder is assumed to be a great team lead without evidence |
| **Horn Effect** | One negative trait colours all judgements | A developer who missed one deadline is labelled unreliable across all work |
| **Recency Effect** | Recent events dominate overall judgement | A star performer has one bad sprint; manager rates entire year poorly |
| **Projection** | Attributing one's own feelings to others | A stressed manager assumes their team is also overwhelmed |
| **Selective Perception** | Filtering information to fit existing beliefs | A manager only notices errors by an employee they already dislike |

### Attribution Theory

Attribution theory (Kelley, 1967) explains how people determine whether a behaviour is caused by **internal** (dispositional) or **external** (situational) factors.

#### Three Determinants

| Determinant | Question | Internal Attribution | External Attribution |
|-------------|---------|----------------------|----------------------|
| **Distinctiveness** | Does this person behave this way in other situations? | Low distinctiveness (same behaviour everywhere) | High distinctiveness (only in this situation) |
| **Consensus** | Do others behave the same way in this situation? | Low consensus (only this person) | High consensus (everyone does it) |
| **Consistency** | Does this person behave this way over time? | High consistency (always does it) | Low consistency (rarely does it) |

#### Two Attribution Errors

| Error | Definition | Team Scenario Example |
|-------|-----------|----------------------|
| **Fundamental Attribution Error (FAE)** | Overestimating internal causes; underestimating external causes for *others'* behaviour | A team member submits code late. Manager blames poor work ethic, ignoring that the build environment was broken all week |
| **Self-Serving Bias** | Taking personal credit for successes; blaming external factors for failures | When the project ships on time, the developer says "I wrote the core module." When it fails, they say "The requirements kept changing." |

<summary><strong>3. Motivation</strong></summary>

### Herzberg's Two-Factor Theory

#### Hygiene Factors (Prevent Dissatisfaction)

| Factor | IT Example |
|--------|-----------|
| Salary & benefits | Competitive pay, health insurance |
| Job security | Permanent contract vs. contract-to-hire |
| Working conditions | Ergonomic setup, reliable internet, remote-work policy |
| Company policy | Clear leave policy, transparent promotion criteria |
| Supervision quality | Manager who doesn't micromanage |
| Interpersonal relations | Respectful, non-toxic team culture |

> Improving hygiene factors removes dissatisfaction but **does not** create motivation.

#### Motivators (Create Satisfaction & Drive)

| Motivator | IT Example |
|-----------|-----------|
| Achievement | Shipping a feature that users love |
| Recognition | Public praise at sprint review or in Slack |
| The work itself | Solving a challenging algorithm problem |
| Responsibility | Owning a microservice end-to-end |
| Growth & advancement | Promotion to senior engineer; learning budget |

> Motivators directly increase job satisfaction and performance.

#### Scenario Application

> **Scenario:** An IT company sees high turnover among mid-level developers despite above-market salaries.

**Analysis using Herzberg:**
- Hygiene (salary) is adequate → removing dissatisfaction ✅
- Motivators are lacking → no recognition, no growth paths, repetitive tasks ❌
- **Retention strategies:** implement mentoring programmes, create individual development plans, give engineers ownership of features, celebrate achievements publicly

### Equity Theory (Adams)

Employees compare their **input–outcome ratio** to a reference person:

```
Own Outcomes / Own Inputs  vs.  Reference's Outcomes / Reference's Inputs
```

| Perception | Reaction |
|-----------|---------|
| **Under-rewarded** | Reduce effort, demand raise, resign |
| **Over-rewarded** | (Often) increase effort briefly; guilt |
| **Equitable** | Maintain current effort and satisfaction |

#### Organisational Justice Dimensions

| Dimension | Definition | Example |
|-----------|-----------|---------|
| **Distributive** | Fairness of outcomes | Two engineers with equal skills receive equal bonuses |
| **Procedural** | Fairness of processes | Performance reviews use the same rubric for all |
| **Interactional** | Dignity in treatment | Manager explains salary decisions respectfully |

<summary><strong>4. Emotions &amp; Emotional Intelligence (EI)</strong></summary>

### Emotions vs. Moods

| Dimension | Emotions | Moods |
|-----------|---------|-------|
| **Duration** | Short-lived (seconds to minutes) | Longer-lasting (hours to days) |
| **Intensity** | Usually intense | More diffuse |
| **Cause** | Specific, identifiable trigger | Often no clear cause |
| **Action tendency** | Strong impulse to act | General influence on behaviour |
| **Example** | Anger when code is deleted by colleague | Feeling irritable all Monday morning |

### Four Components of Emotional Intelligence (Goleman)

| Component | Definition | IT Workplace Example |
|-----------|-----------|----------------------|
| **Self-Awareness** | Knowing your own emotions and their effect | Developer realises they get defensive during code reviews and works on it |
| **Self-Management** | Regulating your emotions and impulses | Tech lead breathes and stays calm when a production server crashes |
| **Social Awareness (Empathy)** | Understanding others' emotions | Scrum master notices a quiet team member is overwhelmed and checks in |
| **Relationship Management** | Influencing and inspiring others | Engineering manager coaches a struggling junior rather than criticising them |

### Surface Acting vs. Deep Acting

| Aspect | Surface Acting | Deep Acting |
|--------|--------------|------------|
| **Definition** | Faking emotional expressions without changing inner feelings | Genuinely trying to feel the expected emotion |
| **Mechanism** | Emotional mask / suppression | Cognitive reappraisal |
| **Example** | Support engineer smiles at an abusive customer while feeling frustrated | Support engineer reminds themselves the customer is scared, fostering genuine empathy |
| **Effect on employee** | Higher emotional exhaustion, burnout risk | Lower burnout, more authentic service |

</details>

<summary><strong>5. Groups vs. Teams</strong></summary>

### Full Comparison Table

| Dimension | Group | Team |
|-----------|-------|------|
| **Goal** | Individual goals; may share a common goal | Shared, collective goal |
| **Accountability** | Individual accountability | Mutual accountability |
| **Synergy** | Neutral or negative | Positive (1 + 1 > 2) |
| **Interdependence** | Low | High |
| **Leadership** | Single, designated leader | Leadership may be shared |
| **Example** | Employees in the same open-plan office | Agile scrum team building a product together |

### Tuckman's Five Stages of Group Development

| Stage | Description | IT Team Example |
|-------|------------|----------------|
| **1. Forming** | Members get acquainted; polite, uncertain | New project kickoff — team learns names, roles, project scope |
| **2. Storming** | Conflict over roles, processes, authority | Disagreements on architecture choices; frustration over unclear ownership |
| **3. Norming** | Agreement on norms, roles clarified; cohesion grows | Team agrees on coding standards, sprint ceremonies, communication channels |
| **4. Performing** | High productivity; interdependence; focus on goal | Team ships features rapidly with minimal supervision |
| **5. Adjourning** | Project ends; team disbands; reflection | Post-mortem meeting; farewell messages; team members move to new projects |

### Four Types of Teams

| Type | Description | Example |
|------|------------|---------|
| **Problem-Solving Team** | Temporary; formed to solve a specific problem | Task force assembled to investigate a security breach |
| **Self-Managed Team** | No external supervisor; manages own processes | DevOps team that schedules, assigns, and reviews its own work |
| **Cross-Functional Team** | Members from different departments/specialisms | Product team combining developers, designers, and business analysts |
| **Virtual Team** | Geographically dispersed; collaborate via technology | Remote distributed team across multiple time zones using Slack and Zoom |

</details>

<summary><strong>6. Communication Barriers</strong></summary>

### Six Barriers with IT-Specific Examples

| Barrier | Definition | IT Example | How to Overcome |
|---------|-----------|-----------|----------------|
| **Filtering** | Sender manipulates information to appear favourable | Developer tells manager "deployment is almost done" when it is actually broken | Encourage psychological safety; reward honest reporting |
| **Selective Perception** | Receiver hears only what they want to hear | A manager dismisses a developer's estimate as pessimistic without analysing it | Active listening training; ask clarifying questions |
| **Information Overload** | Receiver cannot process all incoming messages | Developers flooded with Slack messages, emails, and JIRA notifications | Use async communication norms; limit notification channels |
| **Emotional State** | Current emotions distort encoding/decoding | Team gives poor feedback after a stressful sprint | Wait until calm; schedule difficult conversations deliberately |
| **Language / Jargon** | Technical terms confuse non-technical stakeholders | Developer presents API specs to the board using HTTP status codes and REST terminology | Tailor language to audience; use analogies |
| **Communication Apprehension** | Anxiety about communicating | Junior developer avoids speaking up in all-hands meetings | Build a safe, inclusive team culture; smaller check-ins first |

### Directions of Communication

| Direction | Flow | Example |
|-----------|------|---------|
| **Downward** | Management → Employees | CTO announces new remote-work policy to all staff |
| **Upward** | Employees → Management | Developer raises a technical risk in a sprint review |
| **Lateral / Horizontal** | Peer to peer | Front-end and back-end developers coordinate on API contract |
| **Diagonal** | Across levels and departments | QA engineer in one team consults with a senior architect in another team |

</details>

### Question 1 — Personality & Perception (25 marks)

**Scenario:**  
TechNova is a mid-sized software company. During the annual performance review, the HR manager notices that Team A's lead, Kavya, consistently gives her top performer, Ravi, high ratings across all criteria — even in areas like documentation quality, which he rarely contributes to. Meanwhile, another team member, Priya, received a poor overall rating mostly because she missed a single sprint deadline due to a family emergency.

**(a)** Identify and explain **two perceptual errors** present in this scenario. Use the Big Five framework to describe the personality trait most likely associated with a manager who avoids these errors. *(10 marks)*

**(b)** Using **attribution theory**, analyse how Kavya might incorrectly attribute Ravi's success. Explain the role of the **three determinants** in making a more accurate attribution. *(15 marks)*

<details>
<summary>📋 Show Model Answer</summary>

#### (a) Perceptual Errors & Big Five (10 marks)

**Halo Effect** *(5 marks):*  
Kavya's overall positive impression of Ravi causes her to rate him highly even in documentation — an area where his performance is weak. One positive trait (high technical output) has coloured all other judgements. This leads to inaccurate performance data and unfair reward distribution.

**Horn Effect** *(5 marks):*  
Priya's single missed deadline (a situational event — family emergency) is being used to colour her entire year's performance negatively. One negative event dominates the overall rating.

**Big Five link:** A manager high in **Agreeableness** and **Conscientiousness** is more likely to gather objective evidence before rating, and empathise with situational factors (Priya's emergency), reducing the risk of both errors.

---

#### (b) Attribution Theory (15 marks)

Kavya may be committing the **Fundamental Attribution Error (FAE)** — overattributing Ravi's success to his personal ability (internal cause) while ignoring external factors such as a simpler project, strong team support, or favourable client.

**Three determinants applied:**

| Determinant | Analysis for Ravi |
|-------------|-----------------|
| **Distinctiveness** | Does Ravi perform highly across all projects? If YES (low distinctiveness), internal attribution is more justified. If only on this project, external factors are likely. |
| **Consensus** | Do all team members perform equally well on this project? If YES (high consensus), success is due to the project/environment, not just Ravi. |
| **Consistency** | Has Ravi consistently performed well over time? If YES (high consistency), internal attribution (ability) is more reliable. |

A balanced attribution requires Kavya to gather evidence on all three dimensions before concluding that Ravi's performance is due to internal traits alone.

</details>

### Question 2 — Motivation (25 marks)

**Scenario:**  
DataBridge Ltd. recently conducted an employee survey. Results show that while employees are satisfied with their salaries and working conditions, 60% report feeling "disengaged" and "unchallenged." The company has been losing three to five mid-senior developers per quarter to competitors. Exit interviews consistently mention "lack of growth" and "repetitive work."

**(a)** Apply **Herzberg's Two-Factor Theory** to diagnose the motivational problem at DataBridge. Propose **three specific retention strategies** grounded in the theory. *(15 marks)*

**(b)** Using **Equity Theory**, explain how a developer who discovers a colleague with equal qualifications earns 15% more might react. Discuss **two dimensions of Organisational Justice** relevant to this situation. *(10 marks)*

<details>
<summary>📋 Show Model Answer</summary>

#### (a) Herzberg Diagnosis & Retention Strategies (15 marks)

**Diagnosis:**  
According to Herzberg, **hygiene factors** (salary, working conditions) are adequately met — this has removed dissatisfaction. However, the real problem lies with **motivators**: achievement, recognition, growth, responsibility, and the work itself are absent. DataBridge has focused exclusively on hygiene, leading to a neutral (not motivated) workforce — hence disengagement.

**Three Retention Strategies:**

1. **Career growth pathways** *(Growth motivator):* Create a transparent engineering career ladder (Junior → Mid → Senior → Principal) with clear competency expectations. Offer training budgets, conference sponsorships, and certification support. Developers who see a future stay longer.

2. **Ownership & Responsibility** *(Responsibility motivator):* Assign developers full ownership of a product feature or microservice — from design to deployment. Move away from ticket-based task assignment to outcome-based ownership. Autonomy and accountability increase intrinsic motivation.

3. **Recognition programmes** *(Recognition motivator):* Introduce a peer-recognition system (e.g., monthly "Engineering Excellence" awards announced in all-hands meetings, highlighted on internal Slack). Celebrate both technical achievements and collaborative behaviour.

---

#### (b) Equity Theory & Organisational Justice (10 marks)

**Equity Theory reaction:**  
The developer compares their input–outcome ratio to their colleague's. Discovering an unexplained 15% pay gap (equal qualifications, equal inputs → unequal outcomes) creates a perception of **under-reward inequity**. Likely reactions:

- Reduce work effort to re-balance the ratio
- Demand a salary adjustment from management
- Begin job hunting (increasing turnover risk)
- Psychologically devalue the colleague's contributions to restore perceived fairness

**Organisational Justice dimensions:**

| Dimension | Relevance |
|-----------|---------|
| **Distributive Justice** | Is the pay outcome itself fair? A 15% gap with no objective basis violates distributive justice. Management should audit pay structures for equity. |
| **Procedural Justice** | Is the process used to set salaries fair and transparent? If the company has opaque salary bands with no appeal process, procedural injustice compounds the problem. Implementing clear, published salary bands and a formal review appeal process restores procedural justice. |

</details>

### Question 3 — Emotions, EI & Teams (25 marks)

**Scenario:**  
CloudSphere's cross-functional product team of 8 (developers, a designer, a BA, and a product owner) has been together for 4 months. Initially enthusiastic, the team is now experiencing heated arguments about design decisions, missed standups, and two members who have stopped contributing to retrospectives. The Scrum Master, Amara, notices the tension but avoids confrontation.

**(a)** Using **Tuckman's model**, identify the stage CloudSphere's team is in and justify your answer. Describe what Amara should do to move the team to the next stage. *(10 marks)*

**(b)** Evaluate Amara's behaviour using the **four components of Emotional Intelligence**. Explain how **surface acting vs. deep acting** could affect team trust if Amara chooses to simply smile through the tension rather than address it. *(15 marks)*

<details>
<summary>📋 Show Model Answer</summary>

#### (a) Tuckman's Stage (10 marks)

**Stage: Storming**

**Justification:** The team displays classic storming characteristics:
- Heated arguments about design decisions → conflict over processes and ownership
- Missed stand-ups → resistance to team norms
- Members disengaging from retrospectives → withdrawal, loss of psychological safety
- These behaviours typically emerge 2–4 months in, after the polite forming phase fades

**What Amara should do to reach Norming:**
1. **Facilitate a team norms workshop** — collaboratively define working agreements (communication style, decision-making process, retrospective rules of engagement)
2. **Address conflict directly** — schedule 1:1s with disengaged members to understand root causes; bring legitimate disagreements into a structured forum
3. **Clarify roles and ownership** — many storming conflicts stem from ambiguous responsibilities; a RACI matrix can help
4. **Build psychological safety** — model vulnerability (e.g., admit mistakes herself) to encourage others to engage honestly in retrospectives

---

#### (b) EI Analysis & Acting (15 marks)

**Emotional Intelligence assessment of Amara:**

| EI Component | Amara's Current Behaviour | Ideal Behaviour |
|--------------|--------------------------|----------------|
| **Self-Awareness** | Recognises tension (positive) but avoidance suggests discomfort with conflict | Acknowledge that conflict avoidance is a personal pattern; develop willingness to engage |
| **Self-Management** | Avoids confrontation — impulse to maintain comfort over team health | Regulate discomfort; choose courage over ease |
| **Social Awareness (Empathy)** | Notices tension, so awareness is present | Probe deeper — understand each member's specific emotional state |
| **Relationship Management** | Fails to leverage awareness to intervene and guide | Use 1:1s, facilitation skills, and coaching to navigate conflict constructively |

**Surface Acting vs. Deep Acting — effect on team trust:**

If Amara chooses to **surface act** (smile, perform calm, pretend there is no problem):
- Team members who are already disengaged will sense the inauthenticity
- Trust erodes — if their Scrum Master is not being honest, why should they be?
- Amara risks emotional exhaustion from sustained masking
- The underlying issues remain unresolved, pushing the team back into storming or even dissolving

If Amara uses **deep acting** (genuinely embraces the belief that conflict is a healthy part of growth):
- She can enter difficult conversations with real empathy and confidence
- Team members feel heard and safe to express concerns authentically
- Trust builds because the team sees consistent, genuine leadership
- The team is more likely to transition successfully to Norming

</details>

### Question 4 — Communication (25 marks)

**Scenario:**  
InnovateTech is preparing to migrate its entire infrastructure to the cloud. The CTO has sent a company-wide email using technical jargon explaining the migration timeline and impact. Many employees did not read it carefully. The development team is overwhelmed with notifications across Slack, email, JIRA, and Teams. Meanwhile, junior developers are reluctant to raise concerns in all-hands meetings, and rumours are circulating that the migration will result in layoffs.

**(a)** Identify **four communication barriers** present in InnovateTech's situation. For each barrier, suggest a specific solution. *(20 marks)*

**(b)** Classify each communication example below by its **direction** and explain its organisational significance. *(5 marks)*
- The CTO's company-wide email
- A developer raising a concern about server costs in the all-hands meeting
- Two developers coordinating API endpoints via Slack

<details>
<summary>📋 Show Model Answer</summary>

#### (a) Four Communication Barriers (20 marks — 5 marks each)

**1. Language / Jargon Barrier:**  
The CTO's email uses technical infrastructure terminology unfamiliar to non-technical staff (HR, Finance, Sales). Many employees did not read it carefully — partly because they could not understand it.  
*Solution:* Send separate, tailored communications — a plain-English summary for all staff and a technical briefing for the development team. Use visuals (timelines, diagrams) to supplement text.

**2. Information Overload:**  
Developers are receiving messages across four platforms simultaneously (Slack, email, JIRA, Teams). The volume makes it impossible to prioritise what is critical.  
*Solution:* Establish a clear communication channel hierarchy — e.g., all critical migration updates go exclusively via email; JIRA for task tracking; Slack for team chat. Reduce redundant cross-platform posting.

**3. Communication Apprehension:**  
Junior developers are reluctant to raise concerns in all-hands meetings. This suppresses valuable ground-level risk signals.  
*Solution:* Create lower-stakes channels for input — anonymous question boxes, smaller team forums, or a dedicated Slack channel where junior staff can post concerns without fear of judgement. Explicitly invite junior voices in meeting facilitation.

**4. Selective Perception / Rumour (Filtering):**  
Employees are filling information gaps with rumours about layoffs. When formal communication is unclear or incomplete, people construct their own narratives.  
*Solution:* Address the layoff rumours directly and transparently in a dedicated town hall. Provide factual, complete information about the migration's impact on headcount. Silence breeds fear; transparency builds trust.

---

#### (b) Communication Directions (5 marks)

| Example | Direction | Organisational Significance |
|---------|-----------|---------------------------|
| CTO's company-wide email | **Downward** | Transmits strategic decisions, policies, and direction from leadership to all employees. Effective when clear and accessible; risks filtering and jargon barriers |
| Developer raising a server cost concern in all-hands | **Upward** | Surfaces operational risks and ground-level insights to leadership. Critical for informed decision-making; depends on psychological safety |
| Two developers coordinating API endpoints via Slack | **Lateral / Horizontal** | Enables peer collaboration and technical coordination. Fast and efficient; keeps work moving without management bottlenecks |

</details>
